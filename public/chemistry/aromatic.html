Every reaction here follows the same three beats: (1) a catalyst generates a strong electrophile E⁺; (2) the ring's π electrons attack E⁺, briefly breaking aromaticity to form an unstable intermediate; (3) the intermediate loses H⁺, restoring the stable ring.
// WHY SUBSTITUTION, NOT ADDITION?
An alkene adds across its C=C double bond because that swaps a weak π bond for two strong σ bonds. Benzene could add too — but doing so would destroy the delocalised ring and lose its extra stability (about 150 kJ mol⁻¹, measured from enthalpies of hydrogenation). Substitution keeps the ring intact, so it's the favoured path. That's the single big idea of aromatic chemistry.
