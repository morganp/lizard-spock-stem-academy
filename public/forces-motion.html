// L1 — EVERY FORCE IS A PUSH OR A PULL
Kick a ball, stretch a rubber band, drop a spoon — every time, something is pushing or pulling. Forces have a size (measured in newtons, N) and a direction, which is why we draw them as arrows. About 1 N is the pull of gravity on an apple.
// L2 — BALANCED VS UNBALANCED
Forces gang up. If your push exactly matches friction pushing back, the forces are balanced — and the crate's motion doesn't change (still if it was still, coasting if it was coasting). Only an unbalanced force changes motion. Try it above: on rough concrete, a small push does nothing at all — friction cancels every newton of it.

The crate lab is the second law running live: net force ÷ mass = acceleration, sixty times a second. Double the mass and watch the same push produce half the acceleration.
// L3 — READING THE VELOCITY–TIME GRAPH
The strip at the bottom of the lab is a real velocity–time graph, drawing itself as the crate moves. A flat line means steady speed (balanced forces). A rising slope means acceleration — and the steeper the slope, the bigger the net force. The line sloping back to zero? That's friction winning after you let go. Physicists read these graphs the way you read a story.
