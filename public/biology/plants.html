// 01 — HOW A LEAF MAKES FOOD: PHOTOSYNTHESIS
Inside every green leaf, sunlight powers a reaction that turns water and carbon dioxide into sugar — the plant's food — and releases the oxygen you breathe:
water + carbon dioxide — sunlight → glucose (sugar) + oxygen
// 02 — THE BIG SURPRISE: A TREE IS MOSTLY MADE OF AIR
Here's the part that catches almost everyone out. When a leaf photosynthesises, it grabs carbon dioxide from the air and water from the roots, and rearranges them into sugar. It breathes the oxygen back out — but it keeps the carbon. That carbon gets built into more sugar, then into wood, leaves, bark and roots. Do that for a hundred years and you get a whole tree.
SO WHERE DOES A TREE'S "STUFF" COME FROM?
Almost all of it comes from carbon pulled out of the air — not from the ground. The roots mainly drink water and sip a tiny bit of minerals. If you weighed the soil in a giant plant pot before and after a tree grew in it for years, the soil would barely change weight. The tree didn't eat the soil — it built itself out of thin air and water.

This is also why growing forests are so good at locking away carbon from the air — and why burning or cutting them lets that carbon straight back out.
