Getting in & out of cells

Every cell is wrapped in a membrane that lets some things through and stops others. Oxygen, glucose, water and waste all cross it — by three different routes. Start with diffusion, then meet osmosis (the water special-case) and active transport (the uphill one that costs energy).
// FIG. 01 — DIFFUSION (L2 START): PARTICLES SPREAD OUT
↺ DROP DYE AGAIN
Particles move randomly, but the net flow is always from where they're crowded (high concentration) to where they're sparse (low) — until evenly spread.
DIFFUSION
The net movement of particles from a high to a low concentration, down the "concentration gradient". It's passive — no energy needed. Oxygen diffuses into your blood; carbon dioxide diffuses out.
FASTER WHEN: steeper gradient · higher temperature · bigger surface area · shorter distance
// FIG. 02 — OSMOSIS: WATER CROSSES A PARTIALLY-PERMEABLE MEMBRANE

OSMOSIS
The diffusion of water across a partially-permeable membrane, from a dilute solution (lots of water) to a concentrated one (less water). The sugar can't cross — so the water moves instead, toward the sweeter side.
Balance the sugar on both sides and the net flow stops — the two sides are ISOTONIC.

WHY THE POTATO CHANGES MASS
In dilute sugar (weaker than the cell), water moves INTO the potato — it gains mass and gets firm. In concentrated sugar, water moves OUT — it loses mass and goes floppy. Where the line crosses zero, the solution matches the cell's own concentration: no net movement.
