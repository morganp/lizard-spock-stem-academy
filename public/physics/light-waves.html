// L1 — STRAIGHT RAYS & SHADOWS
Light travels in straight lines called rays. When an opaque object blocks some of those rays, the region behind it gets no light — a shadow. Because the rays are straight, the shadow is the same shape as the object, and moving the light closer makes the shadow bigger. We see things either because they glow (the Sun, a lamp) or because light bounces off them into our eyes.
// L3 — REFLECTION & REFRACTION
Reflection: when light hits a smooth surface it bounces off, and the angle it leaves at exactly equals the angle it arrived at (both measured from the normal — the line at 90° to the surface). Refraction: when light crosses into a denser material like water or glass it slows down and bends toward the normal — which is why a straw looks broken at the water's surface and a swimming pool looks shallower than it is. The denser the material, the sharper the bend.
// L2–L3 — TRANSVERSE vs LONGITUDINAL
Light is a transverse wave — its wobble is at right angles to the direction it travels, like a ripple on a rope you flick. Sound is a longitudinal wave — the wobble is back-and-forth along the travel direction. All waves share the same measurements: wavelength (crest to crest), frequency (waves per second), and amplitude (height). And they all obey one tidy equation: wave speed = frequency × wavelength.

◀ LONGER WAVELENGTH · LOWER FREQUENCY · LOWER ENERGY SHORTER · HIGHER FREQUENCY · MORE ENERGY ▶
Visible light — the rainbow we can see — is just a thin slice of a much larger family, all travelling at the speed of light. Radio carries your music; microwaves heat your food; infrared is felt as warmth; ultraviolet gives sunburn; X-rays see your bones; gamma rays come from nuclear reactions. The higher the frequency, the more energy each ray carries — and the more dangerous it becomes.
