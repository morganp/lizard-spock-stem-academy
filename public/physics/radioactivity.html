// L3 — WHY SOME ATOMS ARE UNSTABLE
The nucleus is a tight ball of protons and neutrons. In some atoms the balance is wrong — too many neutrons, or simply too many particles crammed together — and the nucleus is unstable. Sooner or later it throws out a chunk of itself as radiation to become more stable, turning into a different (daughter) nucleus. We can't predict when any single atom will go, only the odds — which is why decay is a game of probability. (For a refresher on what's inside the nucleus, see Chemistry → Electron Shells.)

// L3 — HALF-LIFE
The half-life is the time it takes for half the unstable nuclei in a sample to decay. After one half-life, 50% are left; after two, 25%; after three, 12.5% — halving every time, never quite reaching zero. Half-lives range wildly: some isotopes last fractions of a second, uranium-238 takes 4.5 billion years. This clock is what lets us carbon-date ancient bones and run nuclear power and medical scans safely.
