// L3 — WHAT MOMENTUM IS
momentum p = mass m × velocity v · (kg·m/s)
Momentum measures how hard something is to stop. A slow lorry and a fast bicycle can carry the same momentum — the lorry has huge mass, the bicycle huge speed. Because velocity has direction, momentum does too: a cart going left carries negative momentum, which is why cart B's slider is negative above.
// L3 — CONSERVATION OF MOMENTUM
In any collision or explosion where nothing pushes in from outside, the total momentum before equals the total momentum after. Add up mass × velocity for both carts, and that grand total is frozen — it cannot change. It's a direct consequence of Newton's third law: whatever push A gives B, B gives back to A exactly. This one rule lets engineers predict crashes, rockets, and snooker shots alike.
// L3 — ELASTIC vs INELASTIC
ELASTIC
The carts bounce cleanly apart. Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. Snooker balls come close.
INELASTIC
The carts stick and move off together. Momentum is still conserved, but some kinetic energy turns into heat, sound and crumpling. Real car crashes are like this — on purpose, to protect you.
