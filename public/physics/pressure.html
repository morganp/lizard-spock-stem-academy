// PHYSICS 11 — LEVEL L3

Pressure is a billion tiny punches.

A gas is empty space full of particles flying about and bouncing off the walls. Each bounce is a push; add up trillions of them and you get pressure. Squeeze or heat the box and watch it rise.

// L3 — THE PARTICLE MODEL
Everything is made of tiny particles, and how they're arranged decides the state. In a solid they're locked in a fixed pattern, just vibrating. In a liquid they touch but slide past each other. In a gas they're far apart and zoom around freely — which is why only gases can be squashed into a smaller space. (Chemistry → States of Matter explores melting and boiling in depth.)
// L3 — DENSITY
density ρ = mass ÷ volume · (kg/m³)
Density is how much stuff is packed into a space. A brick and a sponge might be the same size, but the brick has far more mass squeezed in — it's denser. Solids and liquids are usually dense (particles touching); gases are thousands of times less dense (particles far apart). Things float when they're less dense than the fluid around them — which is why a steel ship, mostly full of air, floats.
// L3 — WHAT DRIVES PRESSURE
SMALLER VOLUME → MORE PRESSURE
Same particles, less room: they hit the walls more often. Halve the volume and (at fixed temperature) the pressure doubles. That's Boyle's law — feel it every time you cover a bike-pump outlet.
HIGHER TEMPERATURE → MORE PRESSURE
Heat is just particle motion. Hotter particles move faster, so they hit the walls harder and more often. It's why an aerosol can warns "do not heat" — the pressure inside would soar.
