// L2 — DAY, NIGHT & SEASONS
Earth spins on its axis once every 24 hours — the side facing the Sun has day, the side facing away has night. It also takes a year to orbit the Sun. The seasons aren't about being closer or farther from the Sun; they happen because Earth's axis is tilted by 23.5°. When your hemisphere leans toward the Sun, its rays hit more directly and for longer — that's summer; lean away and you get winter. That's also why the seasons are opposite in the northern and southern halves of the world.
// L2 — THE SOLAR SYSTEM
Eight planets circle our Sun. The four inner ones — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars — are small and rocky. The four outer ones — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune — are huge balls of gas and ice. Space is mostly empty: if Earth were a peppercorn, the nearest planet would be metres away and the next star hundreds of kilometres off. Gravity from the Sun's enormous mass keeps every planet on its curved path.

Stars form in clouds of gas, shine for billions of years by fusing hydrogen, then swell and die. Our Sun will end as a white dwarf; far heavier stars explode as supernovae and may collapse into black holes. Every atom in your body was forged inside a star.
