// PHYSICS 06 — LEVEL L1–L3

Invisible fields, real forces.

A magnet reaches out through empty space with a field. Send a current through a wire and you can switch a magnet on and off — the trick behind every motor, speaker and maglev train.

// L1 — ATTRACT & REPEL
Every magnet has two ends: a north pole and a south pole. Bring two magnets together and the rule is simple — opposite poles attract, like poles repel. Magnets pull on magnetic materials too: iron, steel, nickel and cobalt. Most things — wood, plastic, aluminium, your hand — feel nothing at all.
// L2 — THE MAGNETIC FIELD
The region around a magnet where it can push or pull is its field. We draw it as field lines that always run from north to south outside the magnet. Where the lines bunch close together the field is strong; where they spread apart it's weak. Scatter iron filings on paper over a magnet and they line up along exactly these lines — and a compass needle is just a tiny magnet that swings to point along them (which is how it finds the Earth's own field).
// L2–L3 — ELECTROMAGNETS
Here's the deep secret: a moving current makes a magnetic field. Coil a wire into many loops around an iron core and every loop's field adds up into a strong bar-magnet field — but one you can switch off. Turn the current up, or add more turns of wire, and the electromagnet gets stronger (try both sliders above). Scrapyard cranes use giant ones to lift cars, then drop them by cutting the current.
// L3 — MOTORS & GENERATORS
Put a current-carrying wire inside a magnet's field and it feels a push — the motor effect. Wrap that wire into a coil and it spins: that's an electric motor, turning electricity into movement. Run the whole thing backwards — spin a coil inside a field by hand, wind or water — and you generate a current instead. That's a generator, and it's how almost all the world's electricity is made.
