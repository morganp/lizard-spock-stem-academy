// L1 — A CIRCUIT IS A COMPLETE LOOP
Electric charge can only flow if there's an unbroken loop from one end of the battery, through the components, and back to the other end. Break the loop — flick a switch open — and everything stops instantly. Conductors (metals, especially copper) let charge through easily; insulators (plastic, rubber, glass) block it, which is why wires are copper wrapped in plastic.

// L3 — OHM'S LAW & POWER
V = I × R|P = V × I
Ohm's law ties the three together: voltage equals current times resistance. Double the voltage and the current doubles; double the resistance and it halves. Power (in watts) is how fast the circuit turns electrical energy into light and heat — voltage times current. The readouts under the circuit are these formulas computed live, 60 times a second.
// L3 — SERIES VS PARALLEL
In a series circuit there's one single loop: the same current passes through every component, but the battery's voltage has to share out between them — so two bulbs in series are dimmer than one. In a parallel circuit each bulb sits on its own branch, gets the full battery voltage, and stays bright — and if one bulb blows, the others keep working. That's why your house is wired in parallel. Switch the sim between the two and watch the brightness change.
// L3 — MAINS ELECTRICITY & SAFETY
The bench above runs on gentle direct current (dc) from a battery. Your wall sockets carry alternating current (ac) at around 230 V in the UK — high enough to be dangerous. That's why plugs have three pins: live (brings the current), neutral (completes the loop), and earth plus a fuse that melts and breaks the circuit if too much current flows. Never treat mains like a toy.
