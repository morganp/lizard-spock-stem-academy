// PHYSICS 07 — LEVEL L1–L3

Sound is air, wobbling.

Every sound is a pattern of squashed and stretched air rippling out from something vibrating. Change how fast it vibrates and you change the pitch; change how hard and you change the volume.

// L1 — HOW SOUND IS MADE & TRAVELS
Pluck a guitar string, bang a drum, buzz your vocal cords — something vibrates. That vibration pushes the air next to it, which pushes the next bit of air, and a ripple of squeezed-then-stretched air spreads outward until it reaches your ear. Because sound is carried by particles, it needs a medium — air, water, or a solid. In the vacuum of space, no matter how big the explosion, there is total silence.
// L2 — PITCH vs VOLUME
PITCH = FREQUENCY
How many vibrations per second, measured in hertz (Hz). More wiggles squeezed into the graph = a higher note. Humans hear roughly 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz; dogs and bats hear far higher.
VOLUME = AMPLITUDE
How tall the wave is — how far the air gets pushed. A bigger amplitude carries more energy, so it sounds louder. Loudness is measured in decibels (dB).
// L3 — A LONGITUDINAL WAVE
Sound is a longitudinal wave: the air particles vibrate back and forth along the same direction the wave travels — not up and down like a wave on water (that's a transverse wave). The particles themselves don't travel across the room; they just jiggle in place and pass the energy on, making bands of high pressure (compressions) and low pressure (rarefactions) — exactly the pattern in the bottom strip above. Sound also travels faster through solids and liquids than through air, because their particles are packed closer and pass the ripple on quicker.
