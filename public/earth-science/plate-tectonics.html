// WHAT DRIVES THE PLATES?
Heat from the core keeps the mantle churning in slow convection currents: hot rock rises, cools near the surface, and sinks again. These currents drag the plates riding on top — and at subduction zones, the cold sinking slab pulls the rest of the plate along behind it. A few centimetres a year, but unstoppable over millions of years.
// THE RING OF FIRE
Around the edge of the Pacific Ocean, plates collide in a horseshoe of subduction zones. This Ring of Fire hosts about 75% of the world's volcanoes and 90% of its earthquakes — from the Andes to Japan to New Zealand. Boundaries aren't random: earthquakes and volcanoes cluster exactly where plates meet.
A rejected idea. In 1912 Alfred Wegener noticed the continents fit together like a jigsaw, with matching fossils and rocks on opposite coasts. He proposed they had once been joined as "Pangaea" and had drifted apart. Scientists mocked him — he couldn't explain how. Only decades later, when sea-floor spreading revealed the mantle's convection engine, was he proved right.
