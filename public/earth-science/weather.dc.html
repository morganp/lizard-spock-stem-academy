Why the seasons change

The Earth is tilted, and it stays tilted the same way all year as it orbits the Sun. That single tilt is the reason we get summer and winter. Drag the Earth around its orbit and watch the seasons flip between the two halves of the planet — then explore what makes each day's weather.

// FIG. 02 — WHY TILT MEANS WARMTH: DIRECT vs SLANTED SUNLIGHT
Summer — direct rays
Winter — slanted rays
When your hemisphere tilts toward the Sun, its rays hit almost straight down — the same bundle of energy is concentrated on a small patch, so it warms up fast. Tilt away and the rays arrive at a shallow angle, smeared over a wider patch, so each spot gets less warmth. Tilt also changes how long the Sun is up: longer days in summer, shorter in winter.
